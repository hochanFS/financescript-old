Concepts


Measures of Credit Risk


credit risk is the risk of losses from a borrower's failure to meet timely and full payments. To quantify a credit risk, we estimate four measures:


  • Probability of default: the probability that a borrower fails to pay interest or principal before maturity;
  • Loss given default: the amount of the principal and interest lost in the event of default;
  • Expected loss: probability of default multiplied by loss given default; and
  • Present value of the expected loss: expected loss adjusted with the time value and risk-neutral probability

We pay attention to three popular models for credit risk: traditional credit model (e.g. credit rating), structural model, and reduced form model.


Traditional Credit Models

click "credit rating & score" on the left panel for more detail


Depending on the types of the borrowers, the approach is called either credit scoring or credit rating. While credit scoring is used for small owner-operated businesses and individuals, credit ratings are used for companies, sovereigns, sub-sovereigns, and asset-backed securities. This approach is widely-used and based on the financial strengths reflected by financial statement or other available resources. These rankings are ordinal. That is, the riskiness is ordered from highest to lowest, but the degree of the risk differences is not readily available.


Structural Models

click "structural models" on the left panel for calculation and assumptions


Unlike traditional credit models, structural models quantify the risk associated with a company's debt. An understanding of option analogy for debt is important. A company's debt liability extends only to the company's assets. Therefore, owning the company's debt is equivalent to owning a risk-free bond with a face value K maturing at time T, and simultaneously selling a European put option on the assets of the company with strike price K maturing at time T.


Since structural models apply option pricing model, the model imposes some unrealistic assumptions similar to those of Black-Scholes:


  • The company's assets trade in frictionless markets,
  • The riskless rate of interest, r, is constant over time, and
  • The asset value at time T follows a lognormal distribution.

Reduced Form Model


However, the assumptions above are impractical. Reduced Form makes less restrictive assumptions:


  • The company's zero-coupon bond trades in an arbitrage-free, frictionless markets
  • The riskless rate of interest r is stochastic
  • Macroeconomic factor X(t) is stochastic
  • The probability of default depends on the state of the economy and is not constant.
  • Each company's default risk is idiosyncratic, which means a probability of either of two companies defaults in a recession depends on each company's actions
  • The recovery rate is stochastic

Credit Rating and Score


Credit Rating


Rating agencies aid risk management by ranking borrowers by riskiness. Standard & Poor's and Moody's Investors Service use 21 rating grades. The ratings fall into two categories: investment grade and speculative grades. The following tables represent Standard & Poor's and Moody's Investor's Service ratings.

Standard & Poor's BBB- and above, and Moody's Investor's Service's Baa3 and above are investment-grade.


Often, issuers (borrowers) pay the credit-rating agency to rate their debt, and the rating agency has some incentive to give higher ratings than they deserve. The ratings are then distributed for public uses free of charge. Also, it is important to note that the agency tends to keep their ratings stable over time to reduce volatility. Overall, thee ratings are easy to obtain, but the actual percentage of defaults on the same rating grade differs greatly over time.


Credit Score


In the United States, the following factors affect credit scores.


  • Paying the bills on time/late,
  • Amount of debt compared to the credit limit,
  • The length of the credit record, and
  • Number of credit card accounts
