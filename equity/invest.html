Return and Risk


Introduction


Investing in equity, in general, accompanies more risk than investing in a bond does, because shareholders' claim is weaker than bondholder's. However, taking this additional risk is often well rewarded by a higher return, and it is a very popular means of investment, especailly in developed countries. This extra return is called equity premium, and each stock has distinct return and risk. Understanding required return (the minimum return an investor requires given the asset's risk) is crucial for equity valuation.


The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)


The CAPM is perhaps the most well-known model for measuring a required return. It assumes investors make investment decisions based on the mean return and variance of their total portfolio. The CAPM evaluates an asset's risk in terms of the asset's contribution to the systematic risk of an overall portfolio during a certain period of time. According to the model, the required return is:


`r_i = r_f + \beta_i * RMRF`, where
`r_i` the require return on share `i`,
`r_f` the current expected risk-free return,
`\beta` the sensitivity of share `i`'s returns on the "market portfolio", and
`RMRF` the equity risk premium, market return minus `r_f`


For U.S. public equities, the "market portfolio" would most likely be S&P 500 or NYSE Composite, and the five years of monthly data are usually used for computation. Softwares like R and Python have made this easy.

The Fama-French Model (FFM)


Unlike the CAPM, the Fama-French Model is a multifactor model, which attempts to describe risk with more than one factor. Eugene Fama and Kenneth French built the Fama-French Model in an attempt to improve the CAPM, which does not consider the size and P/B ratio of a firm for risk and return accessment. The FFM estimate of the required return is:


`r_i = r_f + \beta*RMRF + \beta_s(i)*SMB + \beta_v(i)*HML`, where
`\beta_s(i)` the coefficient of share `i`'s on SMB,
`SMB` the average return difference between small-cap and large-cap portfolios,
`\beta_v(i)` the coefficient of share `i`'s on HML, and
`HML` the average return difference between high-value and low-value portfolios


The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)


The CAPM and FFM are used to compute a rate of return on equity. On the other hand, Cost of Capital represents the overall required rate of return of a copmany. The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) is simply an after-tax weighted average of required rates of return for debt, equity, and preferred equity. The formula for WACC is:


`WAC C = (MVD)/(MVD + MVCE) r_d (1-r_t) + (MVCE)/(MVD + MVCE) r_e`, where
`WAC C` the weighted average cost of capital,
`MVD` the market values of debt,
`MVCE` the market values of equity,
`r_d` the required rate on debt,
`r_t` the tax rate, and
`r_e` the required rate on equity
