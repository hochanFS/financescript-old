Forward Contracts


Introduction


Both forwards and futures contracts obligate the long to buy, and the short to sell, an underlying asset at predetermined prices. At time `t = 0`, the initial values for both short and long positions are zero. However, as the underlying asset value changes, either the long or short position becomes more valuable. The position holder with lower value must pay the value difference to the counterparty at maturity. Unlike futures, a forward contract does not provide protections from credit risks.


Settlement and Termination


When a forward contract expires, there are two possible ways to settle the obligations of the parties: delivery, and cash settlement. Under delivery, the long will pay the full predetermined price to the short, who in turn delivers the underlying asset to the long. Alternatively, under cash settlement the long and short can pay the net cash value of the position at maturity. Unless the contract was nondeliverable forwards (NDFs), the obligation is likely to be settled with cash because of its lower transaction costs.


Or, before maturity, the long (short) can create another short (long) forward contract with the same underlying asset. Then, besides the credit risks, the party is no longer exposed to the changes in the underlying asset values. This process is called termination. When a termination involves the same counterparty engaged in the original contract, then the credit risk is also eliminated.


Pricing and Valuation


In forwards and futures contracts, pricing is the process of determining forward price or rate, which is the predetermined fixed price or rate to be paid at the expiration. Valuation, on the other hand, is the process of determinging the value of the contracts.


A key principle for pricing and valuation is no-arbitrage principle, which states there should be no riskless profit to be gained above the risk-free rate of interest. In order to eliminate arbitrage opportunities, short-selling an underlying asset and then buying the asset later with forward contract should not generate any extra income. Similarly, buying an underlying asset and then selling it later with forward contract should not generate extra income. Assuming there is no transaction cost, the forward price should be:


`F(0,T) = {S_0 - PV(B) + PV(C)}*(1+r)^T`, where
`F(0,T)` the forward price for a contract created at time 0, expiring at time `T`,
`S_0` the underlying asset price at time 0,
`PV(B)` the present value of benefits from holding the asset for time `T`,
`PV(C)` the present value of costs from holding the asset for time `T`,
`r` the risk free interest rate, and
`T` the forward contract duration in time


As shown above, a forward price reflects all cost, As underlying asset value changes, the forward contract value starts to differ from 0. Suppose at time `t=a` (`0 leq a leq T`), we create a new forward contract with the same expiration date (`T`), and let the forward price be `F(a, T)`. Then, the value of the forward contract is the present value of the difference between `F(0,T)` and `F(a,T)`. In other words,


`V_a = (F(0,T)-F(a,T))/(1+r)^(T-a)`, where
`V_a` the value of a long forward `F(0,T)` at time `a`, and
`F(a,T)` the forward price for a contract created at time `a`, expiring at time `T`


Forward Rate Agreement (FRA)


Forward contracts can be divided into many types based on the types of their underlying assets. There are forward contracts for bonds, equities, equity indexs, and currencies. And there is an FRA (Forward Rate Agreement), which is a forward rate embedded in the term structure of the FRA's underlying rate. It is difficult to apply the aforementioned formulas for pricing an FRA, since the rate involved is usually not risk-free.


For example, the lending rate on dollar-denominated loans is often quoted with Libor (London Interbank Offered Rate), which is the lending rate among London banks. Libor is considered to be the best representative rate on a dollar borrowed by a private. (For Euro, the equivalent rate is Euribor.) Libor is a 360-day based add-on interest rate, which menas the rate is added n the face value; in Treasury bill market, the interest is deducted from the principal and then quoted.


Lastly, an FRA has a unique notation. An `a times b` FRA (`a lt b`) is an FRA expiring in `a` months, with the underlying loan being settled in `b` months.


Credit Risk


A forward contract is a private transaction and lacks protections against credit risks. Thus, forward contracts default occasionally. When the counterparty owes the amount unable to pay at expiration or declares bankruptcy prior to expiration, the forward contract is exposed to credit risk. Since either a long or a short, but not both, has net cash inflow to be realized, only one party may face a credit risk.


Futures


Forwards vs. Futures


Just like forwards, futures contracts obligate the long to buy, and the short to sell, an underlying asset at predetermined prices. Though forwards and futures are very similar, the following differences make futures less liable to credit risks:


  • Forwards are private (over-the-counter) contracts while futures trade on organized exchanges;
  • Futures are standardized in terms of the expiration date and settlement policy while forwards are customized based on personal needs;
  • Clearing house, which guarantees a payment, is the counterparty to all futures contracts, but forwards directly connect the long and short position holders;
  • Futures markets are more strictly regulated; and
  • Futures are marked to market while forward contracts are not.

Mark-to-Market


When entering a futures contract, a trader must deposit certain amount to the futures account. This initial margin requirement is usually less than 10% of the value of the underlying asset. Furthermore, when an underlying asset value changes in such a way that the futures account goes down below the MMR (Maintenance Margin Requirement), the trader must deposit to bring it back to the initial margin requirement. This (usually daily) adjustment is made based upon the settlement price, which represents an average of the final few trades of the day. However, at expiration, the long and short settles the value difference based on the last trading price.


Pricing


Since forwards and futures are very similar, the intuition tells us the two prices will be very close. However, the differences between forwards and futures often make the prices differ slightly. First, futures are often considered to be risk-free, and thus only forwards prices will reflect the credit risk. Second, the mark-to-market features make the futures price more complex to calculate. Though the deposit also generates interest through risk-free rates, the risk-free interest rate itself changes over time. When the underlying asset has a positive correlation with interest rates, the long futures position becomes more attractive than a long forward position, because the deposit tends to generate a higher interest when asset value goes up and borrowing for the deposit becomes cheaper when asset value decreases. These complexities are currently beyond our calculations, and are not taken account for the calculations yet.
